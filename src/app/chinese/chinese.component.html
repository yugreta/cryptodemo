Now we'll present actual Chinese ciphers. One early form of Chinese secret sharing split a message into several parts. Each part of the message would then be sent by different messengers at different times. The receiver would then put together the whole message once they received all the parts. If any message was intercepted, the interceptor would not be able to make sense without the other messages. [1]

Code-books were a type of substitution cipher used by the Chinese military. High ranking officials had code-books that mapped commonly used military words to ciphertexts. They would use these code-books to encrypt messages and send them to other high ranking officials who would then need their own code-books to decrypt the message. [2]

Transposition ciphers were also used, where messages would be written in a non-standard way. For example, instead of writing words left to right, they would be written right to left. [3]

While researching, we came across a pronunciation system called 反切 (fan qie). However, we can see its use as a cipher system. In 反切, two characters are used to represent one. This is done by taking the starting sound of the first character and combining it with the ending sound of the second character. For example, 中國(zhong guo) would become "zhuo", taking the "zh" from "zh-ong" and the "uo" from "g-uo." [4]
